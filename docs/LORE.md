# Lore

World lore, narrative, places, and characters for King's Road.

## The World

King's Road is set in a romanticized vision of medieval England. The land is pastoral and sun-warmed -- rolling green hills, stone bridges over quiet brooks, villages nestled in river valleys, old forests of oak and ash. The mood is hopeful. This is a world of quiet virtues and gentle wonder, not grimdark despair.

The game takes no stance on magic or the supernatural. Ancient things may be mysterious or sacred. Whether the Grail exists is left to interpretation. What matters is the pilgrimage itself.

## The King's Road

The King's Road is an ancient thoroughfare stretching 30 kilometers from the farming village of Ashford to the ruined temple of Grailsend. It was built by a forgotten kingdom to connect its settlements and holy sites. The road is still walked today by pilgrims, traders, knights on errantry, and wanderers seeking purpose.

The road is the spine of the world. Everything else -- villages, ruins, monasteries, wild country -- exists in relation to it.

## Anchor Points (Main Journey)

### Ashford (Distance 0) -- Chapter: The Call

A quiet farming village at the foot of gentle hills. Thatched cottages, a market square, a tavern called the Dusty Pilgrim, and an old blacksmith who knew the player's family. Ashford is home -- familiar, warm, and safe.

The player leaves Ashford at the start of the journey. Why they leave is left to the player's imagination. The world is calling.

Features: home, tavern (The Dusty Pilgrim), blacksmith.

### Millbrook (Distance 6,000) -- Chapter: The First Test

A market town where the King's Road crosses the River Mill. Millbrook is prosperous and lively -- merchants, a small market, a chapel of Saint Cuthbert. The first test of character: the player encounters a situation that demands they choose between self-interest and compassion.

Features: tavern, market, chapel.

### Thornfield Ruins (Distance 12,000) -- Chapter: The Old Secret

The ruins of a once-great manor house, now overgrown with ivy and silence. Something old sleeps here -- not evil, but forgotten. The trial of courage: the player must descend into the ruin and face what remains of the manor's past.

Features: dungeon entrance, pilgrim camp.

### Ravensgate (Distance 17,000) -- Chapter: The Tyrant's Shadow

A walled town under harsh rule. The gates are watched, the tavern is expensive, and the lord extracts heavy tolls from travelers. The test of justice: the player must navigate Ravensgate's politics and decide whether to challenge its injustice or find a way through.

Features: town gate, prison, tavern (The Iron Key).

### The Pilgrim's Rest (Distance 21,000) -- Chapter: The Vigil

A monastery and waypoint on the high moor, maintained by a small community of monks. The atmosphere is still and contemplative. Before the final approach to Grailsend, the player rests, reflects, and prepares.

Features: chapel, herb garden, library.

### Grailsend (Distance 28,000) -- Chapter: The Grail

An ancient temple on a cliff above the sea. The King's Road ends here. Whether the Grail is found, and what it means, depends on the choices made along the journey.

Features: temple entrance, guardian.

## The Holy Grail

The Grail in King's Road is Arthurian in spirit, not literal. It is not a magic cup with powers. It is a symbol of what the player has been seeking: purpose, atonement, wisdom, connection -- whatever the player brings to the journey. The ending reflects the path taken, not just the destination reached.

## Recurring Themes

### The Pastoral Ideal

The world of King's Road is deliberately not gritty. Hardship exists -- a village with a poisoned well, a tyrant's toll -- but the dominant mood is warmth, beauty, and the fellowship of the road. This is a world worth walking through.

### Pilgrimage

The journey matters more than the destination. Side quests are not distractions -- they are the point. Every person helped, every moral choice made, shapes who the player becomes by the time they reach Grailsend.

### Quiet Virtue

The game rewards kindness, patience, and wisdom. Not because it preaches, but because the world is more interesting when approached with those qualities. A harsh approach still works, but misses things.

## Names

All names are English, Celtic, or Norman in origin.

### People

Edwin, Godric, Aldric, Wulfstan, Eadgyth, Aelfrith, Oswin, Leofric, Mildred, Hild, Cenric, Dunstan, Edith, Margery, Alys, Brice, Wulfric, Swain, Rolf, Matilda.

### Places

Ashford, Millbrook, Thornfield, Ravensgate, Stonebarrow, Hayward's Crossing, Aldbury, Briardale, Cresthollow, Fenwick, Dunmore.

### The Road Itself

The road has no single name -- locals call it "the King's Road," "the old road," or simply "the road south." Pilgrims sometimes call it "the pilgrim's way" or "the Grail road."

## Tone Reference

When writing content for King's Road, the reference mood is:

- The English countryside in June: long golden evenings, warm bread, the smell of rain on dry earth
- Arthurian legend at its most hopeful: quests for meaning, chivalry as genuine virtue, not irony
- Chaucer's Canterbury Tales: diverse travelers, earthy humor, human foibles alongside genuine goodness

Never:
- Modern cynicism or irony
- Grimdark violence or nihilism
- High fantasy (elves, dragons, magic systems)
- Horror or oppressive dread
